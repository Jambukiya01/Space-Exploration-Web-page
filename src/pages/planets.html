01 About Planets

Moon

The Moon, Earth's natural satellite, is a rocky, cratered world. It exerts gravitational influence on our planet, causing ocean tides. The Moon's phases, from new to full, result from its orbit around Earth. Human missions have landed on the Moon, revealing valuable scientific data and inspiring exploration of the cosmos.

Avg. distance

384,400 km

Mercury

Mercury, the closest planet to the Sun, is a small and rocky world with extreme temperature variations. It lacks a substantial atmosphere, exposing its surface to solar radiation and impacting celestial debris. Its slow rotation leads to long days and nights. NASA's MESSENGER mission provided valuable insights into Mercury's composition and geology, enhancing our understanding of terrestrial planets.

Avg. distance

77 million km

Earth

Earth, our home planet, is the third planet from the Sun in the solar system. It's a diverse world with vast oceans, continents, and a life-supporting atmosphere. The only known planet to sustain life, it orbits the Sun, rotating on its axis, providing the perfect conditions for a rich and diverse biosphere.

Mars

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is a cold and dusty world with a thin atmosphere. It features vast deserts, canyons, and the tallest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. Mars has been a prime target for exploration, with rovers and orbiters uncovering evidence of its watery past, fueling curiosity about the potential for life beyond Earth.

Avg. distance

225 million km

Jupiter

Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium. Its iconic bands of clouds and the Great Red Spot are prominent features. Jupiter has over 70 known moons, including the four Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. Its immense gravity influences the orbits of other planets and protects the inner solar system from some comets and asteroids. Space missions like Juno continue to unveil the secrets of this fascinating planet.

Avg. distance

778 million km

Saturn

Saturn, the sixth planet from the Sun, is a gas giant with stunning ring systems made of ice and dust particles. It has over 80 known moons, including Titan, the second-largest moon in the solar system. Saturn's unique hexagonal storm at its north pole and its many fascinating moons, explored by the Cassini mission, have captured the interest of scientists and space enthusiasts alike.

Avg. distance

1.4 billion km

Uranus

Uranus, the seventh planet from the Sun, is an ice giant with a unique feature - it rotates on its side. This unusual axial tilt causes extreme seasons and strange magnetic fields. Its atmosphere mainly consists of hydrogen, helium, and methane, which gives it a blue-green hue. Uranus has 27 known moons and a system of faint rings. It remains an intriguing subject of study for scientists exploring the mysteries of our solar system.

Avg. distance

2.9 billion km

Neptune

Neptune, the eighth planet from the Sun, is an ice giant with a deep blue atmosphere and the strongest winds in the solar system. Its iconic feature is the Great Dark Spot, a large storm that comes and goes in its atmosphere. Neptune has 14 known moons, including Triton, which orbits in a retrograde direction. This mysterious planet remains an object of fascination for astronomers seeking to understand the far reaches of our solar system.

Avg. distance

4.5 billion km
